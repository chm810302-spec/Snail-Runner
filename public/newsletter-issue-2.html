Running slow, living fully

Your Slow Jog Is Building
a Better Brain

Saturday, April 18, 2026 | Weekly Newsletter

You know that feeling after a run? Your mind is clearer, tangled thoughts unravel, and ideas just pop up out of nowhere.

That's not just a "runner's high." Your brain is physically changing. And you don't need to run fast for it to happen. Here's the science, kept simple and fun.

1

Running Grows New Brain Cells

For decades, scientists believed the adult brain couldn't make new neurons. Turns out, that was wrong.

Professor Erickson at the University of Pittsburgh split 120 older adults into two groups for a year: one walked regularly, the other just stretched. The result?

The walkers' hippocampus (the brain's memory and learning center) grew by about 2% -- effectively reversing 1-2 years of age-related brain shrinkage!

They weren't running fast. They were just walking consistently.

2

Running Fertilizes Your Brain

There's a protein called BDNF (Brain-Derived Neurotrophic Factor). The name is a mouthful, but the idea is simple: it's fertilizer for your brain cells.

A single run makes your BDNF levels jump. A meta-analysis of 29 studies confirmed that even one exercise session meaningfully increases BDNF, and regular training raises your baseline levels over time -- so your brain stays nourished even on rest days.

Did You Know?
People with higher BDNF levels scored better on nearly every type of memory test -- spatial memory, verbal memory, face recognition, you name it.
3

10 Minutes of Easy Jogging Is Enough

Researchers at the University of Tsukuba in Japan discovered something wonderful: just 10 minutes of easy running significantly improved mood and executive function -- the mental skills you use to plan, focus, and multitask.

Their 2024 follow-up went even further: "very slow running" -- the lightest exercise intensity possible -- still activated the prefrontal cortex and boosted focus.

If you can hold a conversation while you run, you're already upgrading your brain. Speed doesn't matter.

Key Takeaways for Snail Runners

You Don't Need to Be Fast

Walking alone grew the hippocampus. Very slow jogging lit up the prefrontal cortex. Your comfortable pace is the right pace.

Consistency Is Everything

BDNF rises with a single run, but regular training lifts your baseline levels too. A little bit every week, for a long time -- that's the formula.

Short Runs Still Count

10 minutes is all it takes for your brain to respond. On busy days, a quick lap around the block is a legit cognitive boost.

While the world chases speed, we're out here running slow and growing our brains. Building new neurons, showering our cells with BDNF, thinking a little more clearly with every gentle step.

Running slow, living fully.
